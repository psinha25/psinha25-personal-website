Couple of Disclaimers

  1. I have very high standards for MYSELF and I expect myself to achieve those standards. As such, when I state that I am bad at taking exams or "fail" standardized tests, it's with respect to my standards. I understand that the scores I achieve are still very good and I'm grateful to be in a position where I can focus on my goals solely.
  2. I am not insinuating that these standards are for everyone. Everyone has their own goals and dreams. My hope is that you simply take away useful tactics for studying for this exam and get a good laugh out of this post.
  3. There is quite a bit of sarcasm in this post specifically. Consider this your trigger warning in case you don't like sarcasm.

My Relationship with Standardized Testing

I absolutely HATE standardized tests, and whether it's considered one or not, I bucket the GRE into that group. Why do I hate them you ask? One reason: I'm really bad at taking them. It's not because I have strong opinions on whether these exams really show merit (I haven't given it enough thought and because I find these exams despicable, I don't plan to give them any more real estate in my brain than the amount necessary to get past them).

Since elementary school, I've struggled with standardized tests. Being raised in Minnesota, I would have to take the MAPS test (no clue what it stands for, don't care either üòÇ), which was held every year or two throughout elementary school and middle school (no idea which it is, don't care to look it up üòÇ) . The exams were broken into two subjects - reading and math. Math was bearable for me because I was pretty good at math. But even then, I would always under perform based on what I'm capable of. I'd be in the accelerated math courses, but still get in the 70-80 percentile (whereas my peers in those same courses would be in the high 90s).

Reading was a whole separate story. I remember in 3rd grade, my parents were worried about me because I ended up scoring in the 21st percentile in reading, which is actually an awful score (in my defense, I'm pretty sure I fell asleep during the exam üòÇ). Regardless, it's safe to say that I hated these exams.

When junior year of high school rolled around, I knew I was in for a treat. After all, I didn't get to just take one standardized test, but four! Colleges just love to have you submit a ton of standardized tests. You got your ACT, SAT, SAT subject tests, and PSAT. Quadruple whammy!

Unfortunately, I couldn't fall asleep during these exams, because they actually played an important role in my future (although I did fall asleep during the PSAT which I regret because I lost scholarship money). I remember putting in countless hours to study for these exams and I hated it. I was bored and had no sense of direction whatsoever. Luckily, I scraped by.

Prasoon, Get to the Point of this Post Already...

In an attempt to structure my studying for the GRE and make the process a little bit more enjoyable, I plan to document my entire journey over the next several months. I'll be writing about what the GRE even is, who should take it, why is it needed, how I plan to study for it, and honestly, provide myself, and hopefully you, some motivation to keep pushing through.

Why Am I Taking the GRE?

Well, obviously I'm taking it because I want to go to grad school. But I wanted to discuss what I hope to do in grad school (mainly for myself so that I can come back and read over this section to instill a little bit of motivation when I begin to die of boredom from studying).

Over the course of my undergraduate degree at UW-Madison, I've come to realize that I have a very strong passion for computer architecture and embedded development (computer engineering fields). I genuinely enjoy learning about these fields and developing new skills in these areas by reading professors research papers, working on projects, and watching YouTube videos. In fact, during winter break, I haven't really taken a break. I've been deeply reading research papers (I've read this one paper 3 times because I will be working directly with the author, Akhil Guliani, starting in the spring semester 2021), working on a couple of projects (check them out on my GitHub), and writing blog posts.

I don't think that my undergraduate degree is enough to get me a role that I want and frankly, I don't want to be a software engineer or have a normal entry level role in the industry and try to work my way up. I really want to make an impact in the fields of computer architecture and embedded system, so my current goal is to learn as much as possible. I want to make new discoveries and improve on the existing technology in the world.

I know I want to get a master's in computer engineering with an emphasis in computer architecture. Currently, I'm undecided on whether I will attempt to obtain a Ph.D. The degree requires a lot of work and genuine interest in the research environment. Luckily, that is a decision I don't have to make right now, but it's definitely on my mind.

Final Remarks

I hope you enjoyed this blog post and got a good laugh at my stupidity back in my glory days. I'm still in the process of figuring out HOW to study for the GRE, but once I create my plan, I will make sure to write about it. If you have any suggestions/tips/resources on studying for the GRE and are willing to take the time to reach out and tell me about them, I'd really appreciate it!
